# Acoustic-monitoring-primates-Ecuadorian-Chocó

## Data from: Acoustic monitoring reveals different responses of two endangered primates to old-growth forest loss in the Ecuadorian Chocó

We applied passive acoustic monitoring (PAM) combined with a deep learning-based classification algorithm to detect vocalizations of two endangered primates—the Ecuadorian mantled howler monkey (Alouatta palliata aequatorialis) and the brown-headed spider monkey (Ateles fusciceps fusciceps)—across primary, secondary, and agricultural landscapes in the Canandé and nearby watersheds.

We selected 163 plots at the centre of which we deployed an Autonomous Recording Unit (ARU): BAR-LT recorder (Frontier Labs) (140 plots) or a Song Meter Mini 2 Li-ion recorder (Wildlife Acoustics) (23 plots), equipped with a downward-facing omnidirectional microphone mounted approximately 1.70 m above the ground. Recorders captured 2-min files every 15 min throughout the day.
Training data included recordings from ARUs and field collections. The vocalizations were then segmented into 3-second clips to train the model with BirdNET Analyzer. To optimize model performance, we applied the built-in autotune function to search for the best set of hyperparameters (number of trials: 50, executions per trial: 1).

Model performance was evaluated on a validation dataset of manually labelled audio files containing 612 howler monkey, 382 spider monkey, and 2,110 background-only clips. Precision and recall were computed from a confusion matrix of manual labels versus classifier output. We set the confidence threshold to 0.99 to maximise precision and minimise false detections. The classifier achieved high precision (macro-precision: 0.98; Tables S3–S4) but low recall (macro-recall: 0.47), indicating that while false positives were minimised, a substantial proportion of vocalisations were missed. However, for the purposes of this study—screening large acoustic datasets to identify sites likely to contain target species—low recall is acceptable given the large volume of audio collected (total: 17,168 hours across 163 sites) and the frequent occurrence of multiple vocalisations within single recordings. For both species, we manually verified detections at each site until a true vocalization was confirmed, ensuring reliable presence/absence data for habitat suitability modelling. This step was critical for spider monkeys' detections due to elevated false positive rate.

See the Methods of the article for further details on the analyses.

## Files and variables
### File: Landscape_metrics_extraction.R
Description: R script implementing the full spatial data extraction pipeline used to compute environmental predictors for both study plots and the prediction grid. The script is organised in five parts. First, it loads study plot coordinates and constructs a 100 m × 100 m prediction grid covering the Canandé study area. Second, it extracts elevation and slope at each point from the Copernicus Global DEM. Third, it defines four reusable extraction functions: buffered mean extraction for continuous rasters (canopy height, Forest Landscape Integrity Index, NDVI), percentage cover extraction for categorical rasters (JRC TMF forest classes), edge density computation from categorical rasters, and road density calculation from a line shapefile (GRIP4). Fourth, it executes the full extraction pipeline across five buffer sizes (100, 200, 500, 1000, and 2000 m), saving incremental backups after each variable group. Fifth, it exports the final datasets for plots and grid as CSV files ready for GAM modelling. Parallel processing is implemented via future.apply. Data sources include the Copernicus Global DEM, the Global Canopy Height Map v2, the Forest Landscape Integrity Index (FLII), NDVI from Google Earth Engine (2024), the JRC Tropical Moist Forest dataset, and the GRIP4 global roads database.

### File: MKY_data_reanalyses.R
Description: R script implementing the complete data analysis pipeline for the study. It covers six main stages. First, it imports and preprocesses acoustic observations from the BirdNET Analyzer output, extracting plot identifiers (Soundbox_ID, Plot_ID, Jocotoco_ID) from file paths, then aggregates detections to unique site–species combinations and merges them with site-level environmental data. Second, it creates the response variables (binary occurrence and scaled geographic coordinates for spatial smoothing) and resolves spatial pseudo-replication by deduplicating observations both by grid cell and by minimum inter-point distance (≥ 500 m). Third, it derives composite variables (total forest cover across JRC TMF classes) and converts edge and road density units. Fourth, it performs a scale-of-effect analysis for both species by fitting single-predictor binomial GAMs across all buffer sizes and variable groups, and exports AIC-based results to Excel. Fifth, it runs dredge-based model selection (up to four predictors plus a spatial smooth, with multicollinearity constraints at |r| > 0.70) for each species, followed by multi-model averaging of predictions weighted by Akaike weights, and exports full model tables, best models, coefficient estimates, and metadata to Excel. Sixth, it generates habitat suitability maps for both species over the 100 m prediction grid, partial effect plots for the top predictors, forest sensitivity plots, a study area map overlaid on the JRC TMF classification, and spatial block cross-validation (k = 10, 10 km blocks) with AUC and Brier score as performance metrics.

### File: MKY_data_reanalyses.xlsx
Description: This dataset contains five sheets.
grid description provides the landscape metrics for each pixel of the 100 m × 100 m prediction grid used for spatial extrapolation of model outputs. Variables include Grid_ID, Longitude, Latitude, Elevation, Slope, and the same suite of buffered landscape metrics as the sites description sheet (canopy height, Forest Landscape Integrity Index, NDVI, JRC TMF forest cover by class, road density, and edge density), extracted at buffer sizes of 100, 200, 500, 1000, and 2000 m.
sites description provides the characteristics of all field sites where an ARU was deployed. For each site, it includes identifiers (Plot_ID, Jocotoco_ID, Soundbox_ID), geographic information (Longitude, Latitude, Elevation), and a suite of landscape metrics: Forest Landscape Integrity Index (FLII) from Grantham et al. at 500 m and 2000 m buffers; forest cover derived from ESA WorldCover, JRC TMF2, and a combination of both products at 500 m and 2000 m buffers; canopy height from Lang et al. and Tolan et al. at 100 m buffers; NDVI from Google Earth Engine at 500 m and 2000 m buffers; distance to water and to forest edge (using both ESA and JRC basemaps); and road density from the GRIP dataset at 500 m and 2000 m buffers. For Reassembly plots, additional attributes describe the surrounding matrix, plot category, and regeneration year for secondary forest sites.
observations contains all detections of spider monkey and howler monkey vocalizations identified by the BirdNET Analyzer classifier across all ARU recordings. Each row corresponds to a single detection and includes the start and end time within the audio file, species scientific and common name, classifier confidence score, the source audio file name, and a manual validation flag (Check): detections marked 'x' were manually reviewed and confirmed as false positives.
observations_filtered is a curated version of the observations sheet, retaining only detections that passed manual validation (i.e., excluding all rows where Check == 'x'). This is the version used in all subsequent analyses.
metadata provides documentation of the dataset contents, variable definitions, and data sources.
